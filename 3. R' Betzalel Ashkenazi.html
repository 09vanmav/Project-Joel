Rabbi Betzalel Ashkenazi

5280/1520 - 5350/1590

Descended from a family of German scholars as indicated by the family name Ashkenazi, very little is known about the early years of Rabbi Betzalel. His father, Avraham, travelled to Eretz Yisrael from Germany and settled in either Yerushalayim or Tzfat, where Rabbi Betzalel was born. Due to a lack of funds, his father was forced to emigrate with his family down to Egypt, where he supported himself as a rabbi and a teacher.

The greater part of his life was spent in Egypt where he was first a talmid of Rabbi David ibn Abi Zimra (Radvaz), who was the chief rabbi of the community. Already whilst Radvaz was still in Egypt, Rabbi Betzalel was regarded as one of the highest authorities in the region and he counted among his talmidim Rabbi Yitzchak Luria (Arizal) (Arizal's uncle asked Radvaz for one of his best talmidim to tutor his brilliant nephew, Rabbi Betzalel was recommended and he tutored Arizal for six years) and Rabbi Shlomo Adeni (Meleches Shlomo). When Radvaz went to Eretz Yisrael, at the age of thirty six, Rabbi Betzalel was elected to succeed him as the chief rabbi of Egypt.

Soon after being appointed, Rabbi Betzalel became involved in a controversy which created quite a storm. The controversy centred around the succession to the title of "Nagid".

"Nagid" was the title of the president of Egyptian Jewry. For six centuries it was borne by a noble family. When the family was no longer worthy of the authority and honour, the succession was decided by the Jewish council of Constantinople, who bestowed it upon one of their own outstanding members in recognition of valuable services.

When the old Nagid died, the council sent Yaakov ben Chaim of the noble family of Talmid to succeed as Nagid. About the same time, Sultan Suleiman sent Mahmed Pasha as his governor for Egypt.

On the first Shabbos, the leaders of the Cairo Jewish community, with Rabbi Betzalel at their head, visited the Nagid to offer him their respects and welcome. Yaakov Talmid was unfriendly towards the distinguished visitors, as though he wanted to let them know that they were subordinate to him. Rabbi Betzalel was deeply hurt, for he saw in the open insult an insult to the Torah. He let Yaakov Talmid know that he had committed a serious offence. The enraged Nagid denounced Rabbi Betzalel to Mahmed Pasha, accusing him and the leaders of Cairo Jewry of insubordination to the Ottoman government.

When Rabbi Betzalel heard about this, he decided that the position of Nagid had become more of a danger than use. He convinced the Pasha that the title of Nagid was undesirable since it referred to secular/political rule and was very much like the title of king, which the Jewish people ceased to have since they lost their state. The Pasha accepted Rabbi Betzalel's view and abolished the title. Yaakov Talmid was now no longer Nagid, but "Gilibi," which means "gentleman," or "sir."

Whilst the authority of Rabbi Betzalel was upheld, there was a great deal of public indignation that it should have been attained at the cost of the title of Nagid. Rabbi Betzalel left Cairo and went to Yerushalayim, where he stayed until the death of Yaakov Talmid. He then returned to Cairo as chief rabbi of Egypt.

When, in 5347/1587, a dispute occurred in Yerushalayim over whether talmidei chachamim not engaged in business should contribute to the taxes paid by the Jewish community to the pasha, and to what extent, Rabbi Betzalel, together with several other rabbis, took the stand that talmidei chachamim, compelled by love alone to emigrate to Eretz Yisrael, and being scarcely able to support themselves, should be relieved from all taxes.

Rabbi Betzalel himself traveled to Eretz Yisrael and settled in Yerushalayim, where he was recognized as the chief rabbi by both the Sephardim and the Ashkenazim. The conditions in Yerushalayim were at this time very critical; and it was mainly due to Rabbi Betzalel's influence that the congregations of the city were not dissolved.

He sent envoys, and even went himself to the Jewish communities in Egypt, Syria and Turkey to raise money for the needs of the community in Yerushalayim. In Damascus he installed regular annual collections for Yerushalayim, persuading the local Jews to set aside Purim as a special day for making contributions, and appointed special gabbaim to be responsible for the collections.

Back in Yerushalayim, the Ashkenazim, who ordinarily did not recognise the jurisdiction of the Sephardim, and who, being largely talmidei chachamim, refused to pay the Jews' tax, nevertheless bowed to Rabbi Betzalel's authority. The Ashkenazim had to contribute one- sixth of the sum that was sent from Europe for their support; otherwise the Sephardim, who were on the verge of extreme poverty, would not have been able to remain in Yerushalayim under the merciless exploitation of the Turkish pashas. This peaceable arrangement between the Ashkenazim and the Sephardim was due solely to the personal influence of Rabbi Betzalel; immediately upon his death the Ashkenazim refused to continue to keep their pledge.

Rabbi Betzalel Ashkenazi is known principally as the author of Shitah Mekubetzes. This work, as its title indicates, is a collection of commentaries on the greater part of Shas, in the style of the ba'alei Tosfos, including much original and foreign material. The great value of the Shitah lies principally in the fact that it contains numerous excerpts from Talmudic commentaries which have not otherwise been preserved.

Rabbi Betzalel designed the Shitah to cover the whole of Shas, but it has only been published on a few masechtos.

Seforim

  1. Shitah Mekubetzes, a compilation of meforshim of Gemara, taken from the works of the Spaniards Ramban, Rashba and Ritva, and from those of the Frenchmen Ra'avad, Baruch ben Samuel, Isaac of Chinon, etc. Particularly valuable for understanding ba'alei Tosfos, because the work contains some of the older and unedited ba'alei Tosfos; besides, commentary of Rabbi Asher ben Yechiel (Rosh) and of talmidei Rabeinu Peretz are partly contained in it.
  2. Klalei HaTalmud, methods and rules for Gemara discussions and psak.
